Double Eyelid Surgery Explained: Incisional vs Non-Incisional

6 min read · Eyelid Surgery

Double eyelid surgery (a form of upper blepharoplasty) creates or enhances a crease in the upper eyelid. There are two fundamentally different ways to achieve it, and the right choice depends on your eyelid anatomy, skin elasticity, and how permanent you want the result to be.

The Non-Incisional (Suture) Method

This technique creates a crease using a small number of strategically placed stitches that anchor the skin to the underlying tissue, without any cutting. It's less invasive, has a shorter recovery, and leaves no visible scarring — but it's best suited to patients with thinner eyelid skin, minimal excess fat, and no significant skin laxity. Because no tissue is removed, the result is generally considered less permanent than the incisional method, and the crease can soften or loosen over time in some patients.

The Incisional Method

This approach involves a precise incision along the intended crease line, through which the surgeon can remove excess skin and fat and create a more defined, durable fold before closing the incision. It's the more versatile option — suitable for a wider range of eyelid anatomy, including thicker skin or eyelids with more excess tissue — and is generally considered longer-lasting since tissue is actually removed and the new structure is surgically reinforced.

Key takeaway: non-incisional suits younger patients with thin eyelids and minimal excess skin; incisional suits most other cases and tends to produce a more durable result.

Recovery Differences

Non-incisional recovery is typically faster — many patients see reduced swelling within a week to ten days, with stitches (if external) removed shortly after. Incisional recovery takes longer, with more significant swelling and bruising in the first one to two weeks, and a final, settled result that takes several months to fully reveal itself as residual swelling resolves. Our day-by-day recovery guide covers this in detail.

Which Technique Is Right for You?

This isn't a preference you choose independently of your anatomy — a qualified surgeon will assess your eyelid skin thickness, the amount of excess fat, your skin's natural elasticity, and your aesthetic goals before recommending one approach over the other. Patients who specifically request the non-incisional method but have anatomy better suited to incisional surgery are often advised honestly that the result may not hold as expected — this is exactly the kind of conversation a proper consultation should surface before you commit.

Natural-Looking Results

Regardless of technique, the goal of a well-executed double eyelid surgery is a crease that looks proportionate to your face and blends naturally with your existing eye shape — not a dramatically different eye shape that looks obviously surgical. Surgeons experienced with a range of Asian eyelid anatomy typically design the crease height and shape conservatively, erring toward natural rather than maximal.
