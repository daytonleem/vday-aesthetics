Porcelain Veneers · Longevity

How Long Do Porcelain Veneers Last?

Porcelain veneers are a long-term investment — and most clients get 10 to 15 years from a well-made set. Some last 20 years or more. What makes the difference is the quality of the ceramics, the precision of the bonding, and how you care for them afterwards.

By Vday Aesthetic Concierge 6 min read April 2026

The Average Lifespan of Porcelain Veneers

Clinical studies consistently show that well-placed porcelain veneers have a survival rate of around 90% at 10 years and 70–80% at 15 years. In practice, clients who take good care of their teeth and had a precise initial placement often find their veneers still looking excellent after 15–20 years before needing replacement.

The lifespan is determined by two things: the quality of the veneer itself (the ceramic material and lab craftsmanship), and what you do with it day to day. A high-grade lithium disilicate veneer bonded to a well-prepared tooth surface will outlast a thicker, poorly bonded one by years.

Shanghai's leading dental clinics use the same international-grade ceramic brands — including Ivoclar Vivadent's IPS e.max, the industry benchmark for porcelain veneers — that you would find at top clinics in Singapore, the UK, or Australia. The material quality is not the differentiator; the cost reflects clinic overheads and volume, not the ceramic.

What Shortens Veneer Lifespan

The most common reasons veneers fail early — before the 10-year mark — are preventable. Understanding them helps you protect your investment.

  • Teeth grinding (bruxism): This is the single biggest threat to porcelain veneers. Grinding generates significant bite force that can crack or chip the ceramic over time. Many veneer patients don't know they grind until they damage a veneer. If you grind at night, a custom night guard is essential — and should be fitted within weeks of your procedure.
  • Biting hard objects: Veneers cover only the front surface of the tooth. Biting fingernails, pens, ice, or hard foods (crusty bread, hard candies) applies pressure the ceramic wasn't designed to handle. The veneer can chip or the bond at the margin can break.
  • Acidic drinks consumed frequently: Regular consumption of citrus juices, vinegar-heavy foods, and carbonated drinks can gradually weaken the bond at the veneer margin over years. This doesn't destroy the veneer but allows bacteria to enter the gap, potentially causing decay under the ceramic.
  • Poor oral hygiene at the margins: Where the veneer meets the gum is the most vulnerable point. If bacteria accumulate there consistently, gum recession can expose the veneer edge — creating an aesthetic problem and potentially compromising the bond.
  • Using abrasive toothpaste: Whitening toothpastes with abrasive particles can dull the surface of porcelain veneers over time. The glaze on a porcelain veneer gives it its light-reflective quality — once dulled, it cannot be restored with polishing.

What Extends Veneer Lifespan

The habits that protect natural teeth protect veneers too — with a few specific additions.

  • Wear a night guard if you grind: This is non-negotiable for grinders. A custom-fitted guard distributes bite force and protects the ceramic surface during sleep. Off-the-shelf guards don't fit precisely enough to be truly protective.
  • Regular dental check-ups every 6 months: Your dentist can identify marginal issues — minor chips, early gum recession, bond deterioration — before they become problems. Early intervention almost always means a simpler fix.
  • Non-abrasive, fluoride toothpaste: Standard low-abrasive toothpastes are fine. Avoid whitening toothpastes and anything with "baking soda" or "charcoal" as primary cleaning agents — both are more abrasive than they appear.
  • Flossing carefully at the margins: Slide floss gently rather than snapping it between teeth. Aggressive flossing near veneer margins can dislodge the bond over time.
  • Avoid staining habits: Porcelain itself doesn't stain easily — one of its advantages over composite — but the bonding resin at the margin can. Heavy coffee, tea, or red wine consumption over years can cause visible discolouration at the edges.

Signs It's Time to Replace Your Veneers

Porcelain veneers don't suddenly fail — they show early warning signs. Knowing what to look for helps you plan ahead rather than deal with an unexpected problem.

  • Visible chipping or cracking: Small chips at the edge of a veneer can sometimes be smoothed, but cracks running across the surface usually mean replacement. Your dentist can assess whether a chip is cosmetic or structural.
  • Discolouration at the margins: A grey or dark line where the veneer meets the gum means the bonding resin has stained or the margin has opened slightly. This is the most common reason for veneer replacement after 10+ years.
  • Gum recession exposing the veneer edge: If gum recession reveals the original tooth surface beneath the veneer, you'll see a colour mismatch — the veneer's ceramic versus the natural (yellower) tooth. This is more of an aesthetic issue than a structural one, but it becomes increasingly noticeable.
  • Sensitivity returning: Veneers normally eliminate most sensitivity because they cover the prepared enamel. If sensitivity returns — especially to cold — it can indicate the bond has broken and bacteria have entered the gap.
  • Veneer feels loose or shifts slightly: A veneer that moves even a fraction has debonded. Don't leave this — once debonded, the veneer and the prepared tooth surface are exposed to bacteria. See your dentist promptly.

What Does Replacing Veneers Involve?

Replacing veneers after 10–15 years follows essentially the same process as the original procedure. The old veneer is carefully removed, the tooth surface is assessed, minor re-preparation may be needed, and new impressions are taken for the lab. Temporary veneers are placed while the new permanents are crafted, then bonded at a second visit.

The good news: because the tooth was already prepared during the original procedure, replacement veneers typically require minimal or no additional enamel removal. The process is no more invasive than the first time.
