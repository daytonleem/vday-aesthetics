Choosing the Right Implant Size and Profile

6 min read · Breast Augmentation

"I want to go up two cup sizes" is one of the most common — and least precise — ways patients describe their goal. Implants are measured in cubic centimetres (cc), not cup sizes, and the same cc volume can look completely different on two different body frames. Here's how surgeons actually translate your goals into a specific implant choice.

Why CC Doesn't Map Directly to Cup Size

Cup sizing is relative to band size and varies between bra brands and countries, while cc is an absolute measure of volume. A 300cc implant might take a petite frame up roughly two cup sizes, while the same 300cc on a broader chest might only shift one cup size. This is exactly why "I want to be a D cup" is a starting point for conversation, not a number a surgeon can order from a catalogue.

What Profile Actually Means

Profile describes how far the implant projects forward from the chest relative to its width — not how full it looks from the front. Implants of the same cc volume are available in different profiles:

  • Low profile: wider base, less forward projection — tends to suit broader chests and patients wanting a subtler change.
  • Moderate profile: a balanced middle ground, the most commonly chosen option for natural-looking augmentation.
  • High profile: narrower base, more forward projection — concentrates volume more dramatically, often chosen for a fuller, more noticeable look on a smaller frame.

Key takeaway: two implants with the same cc volume can look completely different depending on profile — projection matters as much as volume for how the final result actually looks.

How Surgeons Actually Determine Size

Rather than working backward from a desired cup size, experienced surgeons typically measure your existing chest width, tissue characteristics, and skin elasticity, then use sizing tools (rice-filled sizers worn inside a bra, or 3D imaging software) to show you a realistic preview at different cc volumes on your actual body — not a generic model.

Common Mistakes When Choosing Size

  • Sizing based on a friend's result. The same implant looks different on a different body — comparing your goals to someone else's outcome rarely translates accurately.
  • Going too large for your chest width. Implants wider than your natural chest base can create an unnatural, overly rounded look and added strain on tissue over time.
  • Not accounting for asymmetry. Most people have some natural breast asymmetry — a good surgeon factors this in, sometimes using slightly different implant sizes per side.

Bring References, But Stay Open

Reference photos are genuinely useful for communicating the general look you're drawn to, but the most reliable results come from combining that input with an honest assessment of what your specific chest width and tissue can support — sometimes that means adjusting expectations slightly from the reference photo to something that will look proportionate and natural on you specifically.
